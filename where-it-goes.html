Where Your Money Actually Goes

Spoiler: It doesn't go straight to you

Okay, so your loan got disbursed. Where is it? Why isn't it in your bank account? Let me break down exactly what happens to that money.

The Money Goes to Your School First

Here's what a lot of students don't realize: when your loan is disbursed, it doesn't come to you. It goes straight to your school.

Your school then uses it to pay your charges in a specific order. This order is federal law—your school can't change it.

The Payment Order (This Is Important)

Your loan money gets applied like this:

1. Tuition and Fees Come First

  • Tuition for the semester
  • Mandatory fees (technology fee, health fee, activity fee, all that)
  • This happens automatically—you don't have to do anything

2. School-Billed Charges Come Second

  • On-campus housing (if you live in dorms)
  • Meal plan (if you have one)
  • Any other services the school provides that you signed up for

3. You Get What's Left (The Refund)

If there's money left over after paying your school charges, you get a refund.

Real Talk About Refunds

That refund is NOT free money. It's not a bonus. It's loan money you borrowed, and you're going to pay it back with interest.

Use it for actual education expenses: books, supplies, rent (if you live off-campus), transportation, food.

Don't blow it on stuff you don't need. I've seen people do that, and they regret it when they're paying it back years later.

How Refunds Actually Work

When You Get It

Schools have to issue refunds within 14 days of disbursement or the first day of classes (whichever is later).

So if your loan disburses August 15 and classes start August 20, you should get your refund by September 3 at the latest.

How You Get It

  • Direct deposit – This is the fastest (2-3 business days after they process it)
  • Paper check – This is slower (7-10 business days, plus mail time)
  • School debit card – Some schools offer this

If you haven't set up direct deposit yet, do it now. Go to your student portal and add your bank account info. It'll save you a week of waiting.

What If You Borrowed Too Much?

Let's say you get your refund and realize you don't actually need all of it. Good news: you can return it.

If you return loan money within 120 days of disbursement, you won't pay any interest on it. It's like you never borrowed it.

How to Return Money

  1. Contact your financial aid office (call or email)
  2. Tell them you want to return part or all of your loan
  3. They'll process it and notify your loan servicer

This is smart if you got a refund and realize you don't need it for school expenses. Don't keep loan money you don't need—you'll pay interest on it for years.

How to Track Your Disbursement

Check Your Student Portal

Your school's portal will show you:

  • When your loan is expected to disburse
  • How much is coming
  • How it was applied to your account
  • If you're getting a refund and how much

Check this regularly. It's the most up-to-date info you'll get.

Check StudentAid.gov

Go to StudentAid.gov and log in. You can see:

  • All your federal loans
  • Disbursement history
  • Current balances
  • Who your loan servicer is

This is the official federal database. If it's not showing up here yet, it might not be fully processed.

Check With Your Loan Servicer

Your loan servicer (the company that manages your loan) will send you:

  • Disbursement notices
  • Monthly statements
  • Balance updates

Create an account on their website so you can check anytime.

Common Problems (And How to Fix Them)

My Disbursement Didn't Happen

First, check for holds in your student portal. Look for any blocks or to-do items.

Second, make sure you're enrolled at least half-time (usually 6 credits).

If everything looks good and it still didn't disburse, call financial aid. Don't email—call. You need answers fast.

My Refund Is Less Than I Expected

Check your account charges. Your school might have added fees you didn't know about.

Also check if you have any outstanding balances from previous semesters. Some schools will use your current loan to pay old debt.

My Refund Hasn't Shown Up

Remember, schools have 14 days. Count from your disbursement date, not from the first day of classes.

Check your direct deposit info in the portal. If it's wrong, your refund is sitting in limbo.

If it's been more than 14 days, call the bursar's office (not financial aid—bursar handles refunds).
